## Transcript

Most people understand that compound interest makes money grow over time. Very few people understand that the same math applies to attention.

Here is what I mean. When you publish content consistently over twelve months, each new piece does not start from zero. It starts from the credibility you built with every piece before it. The algorithm has more signal about who you are. Your audience already trusts you. New visitors arrive already primed by context they found before landing on you.

The first ninety days of creating content are brutally slow. Almost nothing seems to happen. You are putting in real work and getting almost nothing back. This is not failure. This is the flat part of the compound curve.

Between months four and eight something shifts. Not dramatically. But your reach per post starts to grow slightly. Your saves go up. People start finding older content and following you because of something you made months ago. You did not do anything different. The curve is just starting to bend.

By month twelve, creators who stayed consistent report a pattern that almost everyone finds surprising: their top-performing content is rarely from the last thirty days. It is from months two, three, or four — content that had time to compound. The posts nobody noticed when they went out are now driving most of the discovery.

The hard part is not the work. The hard part is tolerating the flat part of the curve when nothing looks like it is working. Compound growth is invisible until it suddenly is not. Quitting at month two is like stopping an investment the week before it doubles.
